Scars of tectonic extension promote ice-sheet nucleation from Hercules Dome, West Antarctica

Andrew Hoffman, Nick Holschuh, Megan Mueller, John Paden, Atsuhiro Muto, Gordon Ariho, Cassandra Brigham, John Christian, Lindsey Davidge, Emma Heitmann, Benjamin Hills, Annika Horlings, Susannah Morey, Gemma O'Connor, TJ Fudge, Eric Steig, Knut Christianson
November, 2022

Abstract

The geology and bed-topography of West Antarctica has long been understood to influence the ice-sheet’s response to changes in climate. Here, we use swath radar technology to image the upstream origin of large subglacial troughs and basins that feed into several major ice streams that drain ice from the Antarctic interior at Hercules Dome into West Antarctica. Our radar imaging reveals an ancient, marine-proximal alpine landscape host to hanging tributary valleys, large U-shaped troughs, fjord-mouth threshold sills, and subglacial lineations. The landforms we image are typically associated with temperate basal conditions, fast ice flow, and active transport of basal water and sediment. Hypothesized formation mechanisms for these subglacial landforms are geometrically and thermomechanically inconsistent with the current slowly flowing ice at Hercules Dome. This suggests that the landscape at Hercules Dome was shaped by fast-flowing ice when the area may have served as or been proximal to a nucleation center for the West Antarctic Ice Sheet. Recent regional aerogeophysics also show that the U-shaped troughs we image with swath radar feed into larger subglacial basins associated with negative gravity anomalies. The orientation of the basins and 2D gravity inversions suggest the presence of thick sediment columns underlying the basin floor. Extension of the West Antarctic Rift System may have created these basins and promoted ice flow from Central Antarctica to the Ross and Filchner Ronne sectors.
